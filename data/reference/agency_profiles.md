## Overview

This document profiles 10 U.S. federal agencies with AI-relevant mandates, focusing on their rulemaking authority, current AI-related initiatives, and planned actions. The agencies are drawn from the CLAUDE.md scope for the policy-levers project.

The overarching federal context is shaped by several executive actions from the Trump administration:
- **EO 14179** (Jan 2025): "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in AI" — revoked Biden's EO 14110, directed development of AI Action Plan
- **AI Action Plan** (Jul 2025): "Winning the Race" — three pillars: Accelerate Innovation, Build Infrastructure, Lead in International Diplomacy
- **EO on National AI Policy Framework** (Dec 11, 2025): Directs preemption of state AI laws, tasks FTC/FCC/DOJ with challenging state regulations
- **OMB M-25-21** (Apr 2025): "Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust" — requires Chief AI Officers, AI governance boards, risk management for high-impact AI
- **OMB M-25-22** (Apr 2025): "Driving Efficient Acquisition of AI in Government" — procurement guidance

## 1. Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Consumer protection and antitrust enforcement. The FTC's Section 5 authority over "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" is the primary federal lever being used to regulate AI in the commercial space.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Section 5 of the FTC Act**: Broad authority to pursue enforcement actions against deceptive/unfair AI practices
- **Trade Regulation Rules**: Can promulgate binding rules (though this takes ~7 years)
- **Policy Statements**: Non-binding guidance interpreting existing authority — faster than rulemaking
- **Compulsory Process (6(b) orders)**: Can compel companies to provide information

### Current AI Actions
- **Enforcement focus on AI deception**: Actions against Rytr (fake reviews), DoNotPay ("robot lawyer" claims), Evolv Technologies (false AI security claims), Air AI (deceptive business claims), Workado (false AI efficacy claims)
- **AI Companion Products investigation**: Issued 6(b) orders to seven companies providing consumer-facing AI chatbots, investigating advertising, safety, and data handling
- **AI washing enforcement**: Targeting companies making false claims about AI capabilities
- **Dec 2025**: Reopened/set aside Rytr consent order under new Commission leadership

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **Policy statement on FTC Act & AI (due ~Mar 11, 2026)**: Directed by Dec 2025 EO to issue guidance on how Section 5 applies to AI models and when state AI laws requiring "alterations to truthful outputs" are preempted
- **State preemption role**: Central to the administration's strategy to preempt state AI regulations through implied preemption under the FTC Act — legal viability is hotly contested
- **OMB M-25-21 compliance**: Published AI compliance plan

### Key Contacts & Leadership
- **Chairman**: Andrew N. Ferguson
- **AI/Tech page**: https://www.ftc.gov/industry/technology/artificial-intelligence

### Assessment
The FTC is the administration's primary instrument for asserting federal primacy over AI regulation via preemption of state laws. However, legal scholars note that the FTC Act was historically designed to coexist with state UDAP statutes, not preempt them. The forthcoming policy statement (due ~March 2026) will be a critical inflection point. Enforcement actions remain focused on deceptive AI claims rather than structural regulation.

**Rulemaking power**: HIGH (via Section 5 enforcement and trade regulation rules)
**Current AI activity**: HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — the policy statement and preemption posture are immediately consequential

## 2. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Develops measurement science, standards, and technology to promote U.S. innovation and competitiveness. NIST's AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) has become the de facto national (and increasingly international) reference for AI governance.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **No direct regulatory authority**: NIST produces voluntary frameworks, standards, and guidelines
- **Indirect influence**: NIST frameworks are increasingly referenced by regulators (FTC, SEC, FDA, CFPB, EEOC) and are becoming de facto compliance benchmarks
- **International standards leadership**: NIST work maps to ISO/IEC, OECD, and EU AI Act compliance

### Current AI Actions
- **AI RMF 1.0** (Jan 2023): Four core functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. Voluntary but widely adopted
- **Generative AI companion guidance** (NIST AI 600-1): Specific risk profiles for generative AI systems
- **Cyber AI Profile (NIST IR 8596)**: Preliminary draft released Dec 16, 2025 — guidelines for managing cybersecurity risk related to AI. Three focus areas: Secure (AI systems), Defend (AI for cybersecurity), Thwart (AI-enabled attacks). Comment period closed Jan 30, 2026
- **SP 800-53 Control Overlays for Securing AI Systems (COSAiS)**: Concept paper released Aug 2025, collaborative development via Slack community
- **SP 800-53 Release 5.2.0**: Finalized Aug 2025, includes new controls relevant to AI security

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **AI RMF 1.1 guidance addenda**: Expected through 2026 with expanded profiles and evaluation methodologies
- **Cyber AI Profile initial public draft**: Planned for 2026 following comment period
- **COSAiS development**: Ongoing collaborative process for implementation-level AI security controls
- **AI Safety Institute (AISI)**: Status uncertain under current administration — was established under Biden EO but that EO was revoked. OSTP Director Kratsios has signaled continued NIST role in standards

### Assessment
NIST punches far above its weight despite having zero regulatory authority. The AI RMF has become the lingua franca of AI governance globally. The Cyber AI Profile and COSAiS work represent NIST's expanding scope into AI-specific cybersecurity. The key question is whether NIST will receive adequate funding and political support to maintain its standards leadership under the current administration's "minimally burdensome" framework.

**Rulemaking power**: NONE (voluntary frameworks only)
**Current AI activity**: VERY HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — NIST's standard-setting work shapes the entire regulatory landscape. Ensuring the AI Safety Institute continues and the RMF evolves to address frontier risks is critical

## 3. Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Advises the President on science and technology policy. Under the current administration, OSTP is the central coordinating body for AI policy, responsible for the AI Action Plan and regulatory reform.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **No direct regulatory authority**: Coordinates and directs other agencies
- **Executive policy direction**: Issues memoranda, develops action plans, coordinates interagency AI efforts
- **Budget priorities**: Sets R&D priorities via NSTM memoranda to research agencies

### Current AI Actions
- **AI Action Plan** (Jul 2025): "Winning the Race" — three-pillar framework for U.S. AI dominance
- **Regulatory Reform RFI** (Sep 2025): Solicited public input on federal regulations hindering AI development, deployment, and adoption. 793 comments received
- **National AI R&D Strategic Plan update** (Apr 2025 RFI): Soliciting input to rewrite the Biden-era R&D plan
- **FY 2027 R&D Budget Priorities** (Sep 2025, M-25-34/NSTM-2): Directs agencies to invest in fundamental AI research and novel computing architectures
- **Legislative recommendations**: Tasked (with AI/Crypto Czar) to develop uniform federal AI legislation that would preempt state laws

### Key Personnel
- **OSTP Director**: Michael Kratsios
- **Special Advisor for AI and Crypto**: David Sacks

### Assessment
OSTP is the nerve center for federal AI policy under this administration. It doesn't make rules directly, but it sets the agenda that drives every other agency's actions. The key outputs to monitor are: the forthcoming legislative recommendations for a federal AI framework, the regulatory reform actions resulting from the Sep 2025 RFI, and the updated National AI R&D Strategic Plan. Kratsios has testified that NIST should continue playing a central role in AI standards.

**Rulemaking power**: NONE (coordination and direction)
**Current AI activity**: VERY HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: VERY HIGH — this is where the overall federal AI strategy is being shaped. Input here cascades to all other agencies

## 4. National Science Foundation (NSF)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Funds fundamental research across all STEM fields. NSF is the largest federal funder of non-defense AI research, investing over $700M annually.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **No regulatory authority over AI**: NSF funds research, it doesn't regulate
- **Influence through funding priorities**: Shapes the AI research agenda by choosing what to fund
- **NAIRR**: Building national AI research infrastructure that democratizes access

### Current AI Actions
- **National AI Research Institutes**: $100M investment (Jul 2025) in five institutes plus community hub, in partnership with Capital One and Intel. Focus areas: mental health, materials discovery, STEM education, human-AI collaboration, drug development
- **NAIRR Pilot**: 600+ research teams connected to shared national AI infrastructure with advanced tools, data, training, and expertise
- **NAIRR Operations Center solicitation**: $35M, 5-year award to transition NAIRR from pilot to coordinated national program. Proposals due Feb 4, 2026
- **Tech Labs initiative**: New effort to launch independent research organizations tackling technical bottlenecks. Major investment expected in FY 2026
- **AI education**: Expanded K-12 AI education, graduate traineeships in AI, quantum, biotech

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **NAIRR-OC award**: Expected in 2026
- **Tech Labs first awards**: Anticipated FY 2026
- **Continued AI Institutes expansion**: Building on public-private partnership model

### Assessment
NSF is primarily a research funding body, not a regulatory one. Its importance to AI policy advocacy is indirect but substantial: NSF-funded research generates the evidence base that informs policy debates, and the NAIRR is building the national infrastructure for AI research access outside the big tech companies. The NAIRR Operations Center transition is the most consequential near-term action.

**Rulemaking power**: NONE
**Current AI activity**: HIGH (research funding)
**Advocacy priority**: MODERATE — important for long-term AI safety research ecosystem, but not a direct policy lever

## 5. Department of Defense (DOD / "Department of War")

### Mission & AI Relevance
National defense. DOD is the largest single consumer of AI technology in the federal government, with AI designated as a critical technology area. Note: The department now uses "Department of War" as a secondary title under EO 14347 (Sep 2025).

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Military acquisition and procurement**: Sets standards for how AI is acquired and used across all military services
- **No civilian regulatory authority**: DOD rules apply to defense operations and contractors, not the general public
- **FY2026 NDAA provisions**: Multiple congressionally-mandated AI requirements

### Current AI Actions
- **AI Strategy Memo** (Jan 9, 2026): Secretary Hegseth's comprehensive AI acceleration strategy with seven Pace-Setting Projects (PSPs):
  - Initial demonstrations due Jul 2026
  - CDAO must enable latest vendor models within 30 days of public release
  - Strict enforcement of DoD Data Decrees
  - Mandates "any lawful use" language in AI procurement contracts
  - Requires AI models free from "ideological tuning"
- **CDAO realignment** (Aug 2025): Moved under USD(R&E), raising concerns about bureaucratic barriers
- **xAI (Grok) deployment**: Agreement to add Grok models to GenAI.mil platform
- **FY2026 NDAA AI provisions**:
  - Sec 1512: AI/ML cybersecurity governance policy (due mid-2026)
  - Sec 1513: Risk-based cybersecurity framework for AI systems
  - Sec 1533: Cross-functional team for ethical AI procurement frameworks
  - Sec 1534: AI sandbox environments (Task Force by Apr 2026)
  - Sec 1535: AI Futures Steering Committee (by Apr 2026)
  - Sec 347: Commercial AI integration into logistics exercises in FY 2026

### Assessment
DOD's AI posture has shifted dramatically toward speed and scale under the current administration. The emphasis is on deploying commercial frontier models rapidly ("at wartime speed") with minimal constraints. The tension between speed and responsible AI practices (including the "any lawful use" language and anti-"woke AI" requirements) creates both risks and advocacy opportunities. The NDAA provisions provide congressional oversight hooks.

**Rulemaking power**: HIGH (within defense procurement/operations)
**Current AI activity**: VERY HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — defense AI policy sets precedents, and the speed-over-safety posture merits engagement. Congressional oversight via NDAA is an important lever

## 6. Department of Energy (DOE)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Energy security, scientific research, nuclear weapons stewardship. DOE operates 17 national laboratories with world-leading supercomputing capabilities and is central to AI infrastructure (data centers, energy supply).

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Energy infrastructure regulation**: Rules around energy generation, grid operations, nuclear facilities
- **Land use and leasing**: Can lease DOE land for AI data center infrastructure
- **National lab operations**: Directs AI R&D priorities at national laboratories

### Current AI Actions
- **AI Strategy & Compliance Plan** (Oct 1, 2025): Comprehensive plan for AI adoption across DOE missions — science, energy, national security
- **Generative AI Policy**: Released Dec 2025, establishing framework for secure GenAI use
- **Genesis Mission**: Landmark initiative to use AI to accelerate scientific discovery, launched Nov 2025. Collaboration agreements with 24 organizations announced Dec 2025. Open RFIs:
  - "Partnerships for Transformational AI Models" (until Jan 14, 2026)
  - "Transformational AI Capabilities for National Security" (until Jan 23, 2026)
  - 26 Science and Technology Challenges announced Feb 12, 2026
- **AI-FORTS program**: "AI for Operationally Resilient Technologies and Systems" — securing the energy sector for, with, and from AI
- **Data center land RFI** (Mar 2025): Exploring use of DOE land assets to support AI infrastructure demand
- **PermitAI**: AI tools to improve energy project siting and permitting

### Assessment
DOE sits at the intersection of AI capability (supercomputing, national labs) and AI infrastructure (energy supply for data centers). The Genesis Mission represents the largest coordinated federal effort to apply AI to scientific discovery. The energy-for-AI angle is uniquely consequential — data center energy demand is projected to account for 25% of new domestic load growth by 2030.

**Rulemaking power**: MODERATE (energy infrastructure, land use, lab operations)
**Current AI activity**: VERY HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: MODERATE-HIGH — especially on the energy-infrastructure nexus and ensuring Genesis Mission includes safety research

## 7. Office of Management and Budget (OMB)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Oversees federal agencies' budgets, management, and regulatory actions. OMB is the enforcement mechanism for executive AI policy across the entire federal government.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **No direct rulemaking**: Issues binding memoranda and circulars to federal agencies
- **Budget authority**: Controls funding for agency AI initiatives
- **Regulatory review**: Reviews all significant agency rulemakings through OIRA

### Current AI Actions
- **M-25-21** (Apr 2025): "Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust"
  - Requires Chief AI Officers at all CFO Act agencies (within 60 days)
  - Requires AI Governance Boards (within 90 days)
  - Defines "high-impact AI" categories (critical infrastructure, law enforcement, healthcare, government security)
  - Mandates minimum risk management practices for high-impact AI
  - Agency compliance plans due Dec 26, 2025
  - Agencies must update AI policies by May 6, 2026
- **M-25-22** (Apr 2025): "Driving Efficient Acquisition of AI in Government"
  - Prioritizes American-made AI solutions
  - Requires vendor disclosures of unanticipated AI use
  - Prohibits use of agency data to train publicly available models
  - GSA to develop shared procurement repository within 200 days
- **M-26-04** (Dec 2025): "Increasing Public Trust in AI Through Unbiased AI Principles" — implements the "Preventing Woke AI" EO for federal procurement

### Assessment
OMB is the operational backbone of federal AI policy. M-25-21 and M-25-22 are the instruments through which every federal agency must implement AI governance. The compliance deadline cycle (Dec 2025 compliance plans, May 2026 policy updates) creates accountability windows. The "Unbiased AI Principles" memo (M-26-04) represents the most concrete implementation of the administration's anti-"woke AI" posture in procurement.

**Rulemaking power**: HIGH (binding on all federal agencies)
**Current AI activity**: HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — OMB memoranda shape how the entire federal government acquires and uses AI. The definition of "high-impact AI" and minimum risk practices are especially consequential

## 8. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Protects investors, maintains fair markets. AI is increasingly used in financial markets (algorithmic trading, robo-advisors, risk assessment) and by public companies (operational AI that affects material disclosures).

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Securities disclosure rules**: Can require companies to disclose AI-related risks and practices
- **Investment advisor/broker-dealer regulation**: Can set rules for AI use in financial advice and trading
- **Enforcement**: Can bring actions against "AI washing" and misrepresentations

### Current AI Actions
- **Withdrew Biden-era PDA Proposal** (Jun 2025): Scrapped the Predictive Data Analytics rule that would have regulated AI-driven conflicts of interest in investment advice
- **"AI Washing" enforcement**: Settled charges against advisers for false claims about AI use in services — applying existing anti-fraud provisions (Section 206 of Advisers Act)
- **Investor Advisory Committee recommendation** (Dec 4, 2025): Recommended SEC issue guidance requiring: (1) companies define "AI", (2) disclose board AI oversight, (3) report on AI deployment effects. Chairman Atkins and Commissioner Peirce both signaled resistance
- **AI Task Force** (Aug 2025): Internal task force to centralize AI integration across the SEC itself
- **Chief AI Officer**: Valerie Szczepanik designated as CAIO
- **FY2026 Examination Priorities**: AI identified as examination focus — examiners will review AI-related disclosures for accuracy, AI-driven recommendations for regulatory compliance

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **2026 examination cycle**: Active scrutiny of registrant AI claims and AI-driven investment tools
- **Comment letter trends**: Staff issuing more AI-focused comment letters requesting specifics on AI use in filings
- **No new AI-specific rulemaking expected**: Chairman Atkins has stated existing principles-based rules are sufficient

### Assessment
The SEC under Atkins is taking a decidedly hands-off approach to new AI regulation, relying on existing disclosure frameworks and enforcement of existing anti-fraud provisions. The withdrawn PDA Proposal signals no appetite for prescriptive AI rules. However, the examination and enforcement apparatus is actively scrutinizing AI claims, making "AI washing" a live enforcement risk. The Advisory Committee recommendations provide a useful framework even without formal adoption.

**Rulemaking power**: HIGH (for securities markets)
**Current AI activity**: MODERATE (enforcement-focused, not rulemaking)
**Advocacy priority**: MODERATE — limited near-term rulemaking expected, but disclosure framework recommendations are worth supporting

## 9. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Regulates medical devices, drugs, biologics, and food safety. AI-enabled medical devices are one of the fastest-growing regulatory domains — approximately 950+ FDA-cleared AI/ML devices by mid-2024, with ~100 new approvals per year.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Medical device clearance/approval**: 510(k), De Novo, PMA pathways for AI-enabled devices
- **Drug and biologic review**: Can set standards for AI use in drug development
- **Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs)**: Novel framework allowing pre-approved AI model updates without new submissions
- **Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR)**: Takes effect Feb 2, 2026 — aligns U.S. device QMS with ISO 13485

### Current AI Actions
- **AI-Enabled Device Software Functions draft guidance** (Jan 2025): Comprehensive lifecycle management approach — Total Product Life Cycle (TPLC) framework. Covers model description, data lineage, performance validation, bias analysis, human-AI workflow, and PCCPs. On FY2026 "B" list for finalization
- **PCCP final guidance** (Dec 2024): Allows manufacturers to get pre-approval for planned AI model modifications
- **AI use in drug development draft guidance** (Jan 2025): Recommendations for using AI to support regulatory decision-making for drugs/biologics
- **Clinical Decision Support deregulation** (Jan 2026): Eased regulation of CDS software, including AI-enabled products that deliver single recommendations — can now enter market without FDA review if meeting other criteria
- **General Wellness guidance update** (Jan 2026): Broadened interpretation of exempt categories, expanding AI-powered wellness product market
- **Digital Health Advisory Committee**: Met Nov 2025 on generative AI in mental health devices
- **Real-world AI performance RFI** (Sep 2025): Soliciting feedback on measuring AI device performance in real-world settings

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **QMSR effective date**: Feb 2, 2026 — major quality system overhaul affecting all device manufacturers
- **AI lifecycle guidance finalization**: Expected late 2025 or 2026
- **Risk-based AI framework**: Commissioner Makary signaled plans for further digital health deregulation and a new risk-based AI framework
- **Generative AI in healthcare**: Actively exploring regulatory implications

### Assessment
FDA is arguably the most technically sophisticated federal regulator of AI, with a mature and evolving framework specifically designed for AI/ML medical devices. The current administration's deregulatory push is creating tension: the easing of CDS and wellness product oversight may accelerate innovation but raises safety concerns for AI-enabled health products. The PCCP framework is a genuinely innovative approach to adaptive AI regulation that other agencies could learn from.

**Rulemaking power**: VERY HIGH (for medical devices and drugs)
**Current AI activity**: VERY HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — FDA is where the rubber meets the road on AI safety in healthcare. The balance between innovation speed and safety oversight is immediately consequential for patient outcomes

## 10. National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA)

### Mission & AI Relevance
Motor vehicle safety. NHTSA regulates autonomous vehicles and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS), which are among the most visible and consequential consumer-facing AI applications.

### Rulemaking Authority
- **Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards (FMVSS)**: Binding safety standards for all vehicles sold in the U.S.
- **Standing General Orders**: Can require manufacturers to report crash and incident data
- **Exemption program**: Can grant exemptions from FMVSS for AV testing and deployment
- **Defect investigations and recalls**: Can investigate and compel recalls of unsafe AI-enabled vehicle systems

### Current AI Actions
- **AV Framework** (Apr 2025): Three principles — prioritize safety, unleash innovation, enable commercial deployment
- **Third Amended SGO 2021-01**: Streamlined autonomous vehicle crash reporting requirements — focused on materially new information, reduced reporting burden
- **Expanded AV Exemption Program (AVEP)**: Extended to domestically-produced vehicles (previously imports only)
- **AV STEP** (Jan 2025 NPRM): Voluntary framework for evaluating and overseeing ADS-equipped vehicles — safety cases, independent assessments, data reporting
- **Three new FMVSS rulemakings** (announced 2025-26): Modernizing standards for driverless vehicles:
  - FMVSS 102 (Transmission shift position)
  - FMVSS 103 (Windshield defrosting)
  - FMVSS 104 (Windshield wiping)
- **Active investigations**: 33+ opened in FY2025, including ADS and ADAS investigations
- **New Administrator**: Jonathan Morrison sworn in Sep 2025

### Planned / In-Progress Actions
- **SELF DRIVE Act of 2026**: Bipartisan bill (Reps. Latta and Dingell) — first federal statute dedicated to AV safety. Would preempt state AV regulations and require new FMVSS for ADS vehicles
- **Surface transportation reauthorization**: AV provisions expected to be included
- **Continued FMVSS modernization**: More standards updates expected through 2026
- **Staff reductions**: Agency reduced from ~780 to ~575 staff, raising capacity questions

### Assessment
NHTSA is at a critical juncture. The administration is pushing hard to accelerate AV deployment (expanding exemptions, streamlining reporting, proposing federal preemption of state AV laws), while simultaneously reducing the agency's capacity through staff cuts of ~25%. The SELF DRIVE Act, if enacted, would be the first comprehensive federal AV law and could set important precedents for how AI-intensive systems are regulated. The tension between deregulatory speed and safety oversight capacity is acute.

**Rulemaking power**: HIGH (binding FMVSS standards)
**Current AI activity**: HIGH
**Advocacy priority**: HIGH — AV regulation is a live legislative battleground. The SELF DRIVE Act, state preemption, and NHTSA capacity/staffing are all immediate advocacy targets

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Federal Preemption of State AI Laws
The dominant theme across agencies. The Dec 2025 EO tasks FTC, FCC, DOJ, and Commerce with challenging or preempting state AI laws. This is likely to face significant legal challenges and creates urgency for advocacy on both sides.

### 2. "Minimally Burdensome" Framework
The administration's consistent framing prioritizes removing regulatory barriers to AI innovation. Advocacy should engage constructively with this framing while articulating where guardrails are needed.

### 3. Speed vs. Safety Tension
Across DOD, FDA, NHTSA, and the broader federal posture, there is a clear tension between accelerating AI deployment and maintaining safety oversight. Staff reductions at NHTSA and the general deregulatory push amplify this concern.

### 4. OMB as Enforcement Backbone
M-25-21 and M-25-22 create the operational framework that all agencies must follow. The May 2026 deadline for agency policy updates is an important compliance milestone.

### 5. NIST as De Facto Standard-Setter
Despite having no regulatory power, NIST's AI RMF and related frameworks are referenced by virtually every regulator and are becoming international governance benchmarks.

### 6. AI Procurement as Policy Lever
The federal government's AI procurement practices (shaped by OMB M-25-22 and the "Unbiased AI Principles" in M-26-04) will significantly influence the AI vendor ecosystem and, by extension, the kinds of AI systems that get built and deployed.
