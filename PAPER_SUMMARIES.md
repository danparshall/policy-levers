# Paper Summaries

### Jones (2024) — "The AI Dilemma: Growth versus Existential Risk"
**File:** `Jones_2024__AI_dilemma_growth_vs_risk.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Jones_2024__AI_dilemma_growth_vs_risk.txt` | **Pages:** 11 | *AER: Insights 6(4): 575–590; NBER WP 31837*

Formalizes the trade-off between AI-driven economic growth and existential risk. The model is deliberately simple: using AI for *T* periods raises consumption to c₀e^(gT) but survival probability drops to e^(−δT). The optimal intensity of AI use depends critically on the curvature of the utility function. With log utility (γ = 1), the model is remarkably risk-tolerant — agents would accept up to a one-in-three chance of extinction for 10% annual growth. With CRRA utility at γ ≥ 2, the picture reverses sharply: because utility is bounded and marginal utility falls rapidly, even infinite consumption generates finite utility, making large existential gambles unacceptable — the tolerable extinction probability drops to just 4% at γ=2 and 2% at γ=3 (Table 1, with δ=1%/yr and g=10%).

A key exception: if AI also extends life expectancy, these mortality reductions are "in the same units" as existential risk and don't run into declining marginal utility of consumption, making larger risks bearable even at high risk aversion. The paper also considers singularity scenarios (self-improving AI generating accelerating growth) and finds the bounded-utility result holds — infinite consumption is worth only finite utility. The paper also considers optimal policy responses, arguing for a large tax on AI compute to fund safety research and slow the race. This is the "Oppenheimer question" paper referenced in Jones (2026) — which extends these results to estimate that society is underinvesting in AI safety by a factor of 30 or more — and provides the formal welfare framework for evaluating whether AI development should be slowed or halted on safety grounds.

### Kording & Marinescu (2025) — "(Artificial) Intelligence Saturation and the Future of Work"
**File:** `Kording_2025__AI_saturation_future_of_work.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Kording_2025__AI_saturation_future_of_work.txt` | **Pages:** 50 | *Brookings Center on Regulation and Markets Working Paper, November 2025*

Bridges the gap between AI-expert predictions of explosive growth and economists' predictions of modest impact by separating production into physical and intelligence sectors within a nested CES framework. The key insight is "intelligence saturation": because physical and intelligence outputs are complements (calibrated using Oberfield & Raval's manufacturing elasticity), the marginal returns to intelligence capital saturate no matter how fast AI scales. AI replaces humans in intelligence tasks first (since AI capital costs fall much faster than physical capital costs), pushing workers toward the physical sector. The wage effect is theoretically ambiguous and can be non-monotonic — in the baseline calibration, wages first increase (productivity gains dominate) then decrease (reallocation into physical sector dominates) as automation progresses. A necessary condition for wages to fall is that the employment share in the intelligence sector decreases; this condition is not sufficient because automation can raise output enough to offset the reallocation effect.

The paper provides an interactive simulation tool (code on GitHub) showing how parameter changes shift wage and output trajectories. Key parameter sensitivities: stronger decreasing returns in intelligence dampen positive wage effects; lower within-intelligence task substitutability produces a more pronounced wage hump; higher physical-intelligence substitutability makes wage effects more negative at high automation but output effects more positive. Under rapid AI capital doubling (every 6 months), wages rise monotonically during automation and saturate after full intelligence-task automation — the hump disappears. After full automation, more AI and physical capital both increase wages, but with complementary sectors, the marginal wage impact of AI capital saturates as AI grows large. The framework nests both the economist view (bounded effects from complementarity) and the AI-expert view (explosive growth from high substitutability) as special cases within a shared parameter space, making the debate empirically resolvable.

### Mertens, Thompson et al. (2026) — "Crashing Waves vs. Rising Tides"
**File:** `Mertens_2026__crashing_waves_rising_tides_AI_automation.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Mertens_2026__crashing_waves_rising_tides_AI_automation.txt` | arXiv: 2604.01363 | MIT FutureTech

Preliminary findings from an ongoing large-scale evaluation of LLM capabilities across 3,000+ O*NET-derived tasks, with 17,205 evaluations by domain experts rating outputs from 41 LLMs. The central finding is that AI automation follows a "rising tide" pattern — the logistic relationship between AI success and task duration is surprisingly flat (slope = -0.31 in log-odds), meaning AI performs not dramatically differently on short vs. long tasks. This contrasts with METR's "crashing wave" finding on deterministic software-engineering tasks. At the "minimally sufficient without edits" threshold (score >= 7), the average success rate across all models is 60%, with frontier models in 2024-Q3 already achieving 50% success on tasks taking humans a full day. Success rates improve by 8-11 percentage points annually; the implied doubling time for feasible task duration at a given success rate is 3.8 months.

Key findings: (1) Newer models produce approximately parallel upward shifts in the success-duration curve (beta = -0.31 vs -0.32), meaning improvement is broad-based rather than concentrated on short tasks — consistent with a capability frontier that advances uniformly. (2) Larger models show an outward rotation (beta = -0.36 vs -0.26), gaining more on short tasks. (3) Success-duration slopes vary sharply by O*NET job family: Installation/Maintenance/Repair has highest success (73%) with near-zero slope, while Legal has lowest (47%); only ~25% of families show significantly negative slopes. (4) Extrapolation suggests 80-95% success on most text-based tasks by 2029 at minimally sufficient quality, but near-perfect performance takes much longer due to logistic tail behavior. Important caveats: data collection ongoing, text-only tasks, survey provides all needed information (no real-world integration friction), and "last-mile" deployment costs not captured.

### METR (2025) — "Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks"
**File:** `METR_2025__long_tasks_frontier.pdf` | **Text:** `text/METR_2025__long_tasks_frontier.txt` | **Pages:** 45

Proposes the "50%-task-completion time horizon" as a metric: the duration of tasks that AI models can complete with 50% success rate, measured against skilled human baselines. Using 170 tasks across RE-Bench, HCAST, and a new suite of shorter software tasks (SWAA), the authors find that frontier models (as of Claude 3.7 Sonnet) have a 50% time horizon of ~50 minutes. The time horizon has been doubling approximately every 7 months since 2019, though the trend may have accelerated in 2024.

The increase is driven primarily by greater reliability, better error recovery, improved logical reasoning, and enhanced tool use. If the trend generalizes to real-world software tasks, extrapolation predicts AI systems capable of automating many software tasks that currently take humans a month within ~5 years. The paper carefully discusses limitations including external validity and the implications of increased autonomy for dangerous capabilities. This is the best current framework for translating benchmark scores into economically meaningful capability measures.

### Parshall & Lopez-Luzuriaga (2026) — "Measuring AI's Economic Reach: A Multi-Dimensional Task Taxonomy"
**File:** `Parshall_Lopez-Luzuriaga_TaskExposure_2026.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Parshall_Lopez-Luzuriaga_TaskExposure_2026.txt` | **Pages:** 49 | *Canary Institute / GWU Center for Economic Research, 2026*

Proposes **CDR**, a three-axis ordinal taxonomy that separates AI exposure into Cognitive complexity (C0–C4), Deployment difficulty (D0–D4), and Regulatory restrictions (R0–R4), extending Autor (2003)'s routine/non-routine × cognitive/manual matrix into a finer 5×5 cognitive–embodiment plane with a regulatory third axis. The core argument is that existing single-score exposure measures (Eloundou, Felten) conflate three dimensions that advance at structurally different rates — AI capability (fast, per METR), physical deployment (medium), and institutional/regulatory barriers (slow) — producing the disagreement Gimbel et al. (2026) observe across metrics for the most-exposed occupations.

Methodological contribution: **Detailed Work Activity (DWA) decomposition** resolves the compound-task problem in O*NET (e.g., "diagnose and repair heating systems" splits into a C2/D0 diagnostic and a C1/D3 repair). Applied via multi-model LLM consensus (Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5-mini, Gemini 3 Flash; validated against flagships) to the full O*NET universe: **23,850 DWA-task pairs across 923 occupations**. Headline findings: **40.2% of U.S. economy-weighted labor time** is in tasks within current AI cognitive reach (C≤2), requiring no physical infrastructure (D0), and facing no meaningful regulatory barrier (R<2). An additional **19.6%** is cognitively/physically accessible but blocked by regulation (R2 professional standards 11.5%; R3 statutory 8.0%; R4 moral agency 0.1%). Validated against four external datasets: Eloundou human exposure ratings, Carollo licensure data, Career OneStop licensing records, BLS fatal-injury rates. Framework is positioned as a longitudinal instrument: ~$100 per three-model re-run, ~$200 with flagships.

Theoretical motivation draws from Jones & Tonetti (2026) weak-links intuition: under non-linear complementarity, the slowest-advancing CDR axis binds. Structural prediction: as C advances, the binding constraint for more tasks shifts from cognition to deployment/regulation — a prediction single-axis frameworks cannot generate. Consistent with Massenkoff & McCrory (2026), who find 94% theoretical feasibility vs 33% actual Claude deployment for Computer & Mathematical tasks. This paper is framed as the first in a planned research program; companion work will cover adoption dynamics, an F-axis (failure consequences) and verification, and developing-country applications where D0 tasks become tradable.

## Advocacy & Position Documents

Primary-source operational and position documents on AI-policy engagement, not academic papers. Two orgs: **ControlAI** (UK-based, now US-expanding; superintelligence-prohibition ask) and **CAIP** (US, Congress-focused, audit-based model legislation). ### Leahy, Alfour, Scammell, Miotti, Shimi (2024) — "The Compendium"
**File:** `Leahy_2024__the_compendium.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Leahy_2024__the_compendium.txt` | **Pages:** 113 | *V1.3.1, 9 Dec 2024 — thecompendium.ai* | Connor Leahy, Gabriel Alfour, Chris Scammell, Andrea Miotti, Adam Shimi (Conjecture / ControlAI)

The canonical worldview-grounding document for the ControlAI / Conjecture position on AGI extinction risk, aimed at lay-readable technical depth. Seven substantive sections: (1) The state of AI today — frames current progress as resource-driven rather than insight-driven and characterizes AI systems as "grown, not built"; (2) Intelligence — mechanistic model; (3) AI Catastrophe — argues the default trajectory is AGI → ASI → "godlike AI" → extinction, without intervention at each stage; (4) AI Safety — argues current technical alignment work is not on track and "AI will not solve alignment for us"; (5) AI Governance — argues current governance work is not on track and in fact endorses the race to AGI by focusing on capability-distribution rather than capability-prevention; (6) The AI Race — an ideological taxonomy (Utopists, Accelerationists, Zealots, Opportunists, Big Tech) arguing the race is driven by belief systems, not economic inevitability; (7) A good future — civic engagement as the necessary response. The document is the intellectual basis for ControlAI's policy ask: because AGI/ASI cannot be developed safely and cannot be reliably distinguished from beneficial systems prior to deployment, the correct policy response is prohibition of frontier development until controllability is demonstrated. Reading this is a prerequisite for any serious engagement with ControlAI — their careers page names it explicitly. For Canary, the point of divergence from "analytical-shop neutrality" is here: the Compendium is assertive about conclusions ("godlike AI leads to extinction"), not agnostic about risk levels.

### Miotti, Bilge, Kasten, Newport (2025) — "A Narrow Path: How to Secure Our Future"
**File:** `ControlAI_2025__narrow_path.pdf` | **Text:** `text/ControlAI_2025__narrow_path.txt` | **Pages:** 83 | *April 2025 — narrowpath.co* | Andrea Miotti, Tolga Bilge, Dave Kasten, James Newport (ControlAI; acknowledgements to Aguirre, Leahy, Tegmark, García Martínez, Alfour, Shimi, others)

ControlAI's canonical affirmative policy proposal — the plan that backs the prohibition ask. Three phases: **Phase 0 (Safety)** — six domestic-policy measures intended to prevent ASI development for 20 years: (1) prohibit ASI development directly, (2) prohibit AI capable of breaking out of its environment, (3) prohibit AIs that improve other AIs (recursive self-improvement), (4) require a valid safety case for deployment, (5) a licensing regime with restrictions on general intelligence of AI systems, (6) an international treaty establishing common redlines; **Phase 1 (Stability)** — three international institutions: an International AI Safety Commission (IASC), a Global Unit for AI Research and Development (GUARD), and an International AI Tribunal (IAT); **Phase 2 (Flourishing)** — science and metrology of intelligence, safe-by-design engineering to enable transformative AI under human control after the moratorium. This is what ControlAI is actively pushing to UK and US lawmakers via their Direct Institutional Plan; the UK-parliament campaign endorsed by 100+ lawmakers is effectively an endorsement of this framework. For anyone evaluating a role with ControlAI, this is the policy program you'd be promoting — the prohibition frame is not negotiable around the edges, it's the scaffolding.

### García (2025) — "What We Learned from Briefing 70+ Lawmakers on the Threat from AI"
**File:** `Garcia_2025__controlai_70_lawmakers.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Garcia_2025__controlai_70_lawmakers.txt` | **Pages:** 22 | *LessWrong, 27 May 2025 (linkpost from Substack)* | Leticia García Martínez (Policy Advisor, ControlAI)

Post-mortem on ~70 cross-party UK-parliament briefings between late 2024 and mid-May 2025 (roughly ⅓ MPs, ⅓ Lords, ⅓ devolved legislators, plus 8 staffer-only meetings). The central methodological moves: open with authority-figure framing (Nobel laureates and AI-lab CEOs on extinction risk) before presenting concerns; bring printed media articles and short video explainers (e.g., Apollo Research's 2-minute AI-scheming video) rather than research papers; deliver a concrete behavioral ask alongside information — support for ControlAI's public campaign statement. **One in three parliamentarians briefed endorsed that statement**, treated as the core "tangible signal" metric because politeness-bias makes verbal feedback unreliable. Other key lessons: 80–85% of parliamentarians had minimal AI literacy; cold outreach outperformed targeting AI-interested MPs; relentless follow-up is non-insulting; common failure modes include conflating AI consciousness with harm, defeatism from social-media moderation analogies, and China-competition counter-framing. Closest thing in the literature to a reproducible lawmaker-briefing playbook, and the methodological template ControlAI is now scaling to the US, Canada, and Germany.

## Independent Position Essays

Influential individual-author manifestos that shape current AI-policy discourse without being products of an advocacy org. Distinct from the org-affiliated material above because the policy ask (where there is one) is the author's own and the conflicts of interest are individual rather than institutional.

### Aschenbrenner (2024) — "Situational Awareness: The Decade Ahead"
**File:** `Aschenbrenner_2024__situational_awareness.pdf` | **Text:** `text/Aschenbrenner_2024__situational_awareness.txt` | **Pages:** 165 | *June 2024 — situational-awareness.ai* | Leopold Aschenbrenner (formerly OpenAI Superalignment team, fired April 2024 over a disputed information-leak allegation; subsequently founded Situational Awareness LP, an AGI-thesis hedge fund managing >$1.5B AUM as of mid-2025)

The most-cited AGI-trajectory manifesto in current Hill discourse, structured as a 50,000-word five-part essay series. **Section I (Counting the OOMs)** argues AGI by 2027 is "strikingly plausible" by trusting trendlines on three orthogonal scaling axes: physical compute (~0.5 OOM/year), algorithmic efficiency (~0.5 OOM/year), and "unhobbling" gains (chain-of-thought, tool use, longer context, agentic scaffolding) producing chatbot-to-agent step-changes. **Section II (Intelligence Explosion)** argues that once AGI automates AI research, hundreds of millions of AGI-instances at 10x+ human speed could compress 5+ OOMs of algorithmic progress into ≤1 year, taking the world from human-level to vastly superhuman AI in months — "no sci-fi required." **Sections IIIa–IIId** pivot to geopolitics: trillion-dollar compute/power buildout (IIIa); US lab security as an existential national-security failure where "we are basically handing superintelligence to the CCP" because labs treat AGI weights "the way we would random SaaS software" (IIIb); superalignment as a real but solvable problem where "things could very easily go off the rails" but "we'll muddle through" (IIIc); and "the free world must prevail" — the argument that a US AGI lead is required for both safety (margin of error during a tight race) and survival (vs the CCP, vs authoritarian global dominance) (IIId). **Section IV (The Project)** predicts a national-security takeover of AGI development by 2027/28, parallel to the Manhattan Project, on the grounds that "no startup can handle superintelligence" — the document's most concrete policy prediction. **Section V (Parting Thoughts)** closes with "AGI realism," positioning Aschenbrenner between "doomers" (whom he characterizes as ossified and untethered from deep-learning empirics) and e/acc accelerationists.

The piece is structurally closer to George Kennan's 1946 "Long Telegram" than to a research paper — empirical claims pressed into service for a strategic frame. The trendline arguments have largely held through April 2026: METR (2025, this library) measures the 50%-task-completion time horizon doubling every ~7 months; Mertens et al. (2026, this library) finds frontier models at 50% success on full-workday tasks by late 2024 — both broadly consistent with Aschenbrenner's pace projections. The "Project" predictions are partially borne out by the December 2025 federal-preemption EO, the Stargate buildout, and the Trump-administration AI Action Plan, though in a form Aschenbrenner did not specify (preemption rather than nationalization) and pointing in a direction (less regulation, faster capex) that diverges from the cautious-stewardship version he sketched. Several premises are asserted rather than argued and stand in direct tension with positions elsewhere in this library: (a) **alignment solvability** is assumed without engaging the Compendium's argument that controllability cannot be demonstrated prior to deployment, or Jones (2024)'s formal result that under standard CRRA risk-aversion the welfare-tolerable extinction probability is ~4% — well below Hinton/Amodei's stated estimates of 10–25%; (b) the geopolitical frame **collapses safety and acceleration** (a US lead is presented as both safety-imperative and economic-imperative), eliding the "racing causes safety to be deprioritized" tension that motivates the Hawley-Blumenthal AIRE Act and the Narrow Path moratorium; (c) **the "doomer" dismissal** is rhetorical, not argumentative, but has propagated through Aschenbrenner's readership in policy circles as a shorthand reason to ignore slowdown advocacy.

**Conflict of interest worth surfacing in any citation:** Aschenbrenner founded Situational Awareness LP shortly after the essay's release — a long/short equity hedge fund explicitly built on the essay's thesis (AGI-driven capex into compute, power, semiconductors, infrastructure), seeded by Patrick & John Collison, Nat Friedman, and Daniel Gross. As of late 2025 the fund managed ~$1.5B AUM with ~$5.5B in U.S. equity exposure, returned ~47% in H1 2025, and Aschenbrenner has reportedly invested nearly all his personal net worth in it. The thesis and the fund are explicitly co-promoted (the launch was paired with a four-hour Dwarkesh Patel podcast in which Aschenbrenner stated: "if AGI were priced in tomorrow, you could maybe make 100x"). The fund's performance depends on short AGI timelines, large AI-infrastructure capex, and regulation that doesn't slow either. This does not invalidate any argument in the essay, but it materially affects how the document's policy recommendations (oppose pauses, oppose regulation that slows capex) should be weighted.

**Operational note for Hill outreach:** This is the load-bearing reference for the "we must race" framing staffers cite when opposing pre-deployment evaluation or state-level regulation. The most productive counter is not to dispute the trendlines (which are roughly correct) but to point at (i) Jones (2024) — the trendlines being right makes the case for slowdown *stronger*, not weaker, under any reasonable risk-aversion assumption; (ii) Kording & Marinescu (2025) — the "shared prosperity from AGI" frame omits the wage hump-then-fall trajectory their model produces; (iii) Leahy 2024 / Miotti 2025 — the same trendlines support the opposite policy conclusion (prohibition until controllability is demonstrated). For senators on Armed Services, Intelligence, or Foreign Relations committees, the "Free World" framing is likely to have been pre-absorbed by their AI staff.
