# USCC Commissioners: 2024 Report Cycle and 2026 Report Cycle

Companion to `us_china_council/PAPER_SUMMARIES.md`. Tracks the 12 commissioners who unanimously approved the November 19, 2024 Annual Report, and the 12 commissioners (5 carryovers, 7 new) on the 2026 Report Cycle as of April 2026.

**Why this distinction matters for advocacy:** The 2024 commission produced the AGI Manhattan Project recommendation. The 2026 commission inherits that recommendation as part of the institutional record but is not bound by it — they may refine, soften, escalate, or replace it in their own report. Understanding which commissioners voted on which reports, and what their stated positions are, lets you anticipate where the institution is heading rather than treating "USCC says X" as static. This is also where Anthropic's own institutional connection to the commission lives: Mike Kuiken, currently 2026 Vice Chair, is a member of Anthropic's National Security and Public Sector Advisory Council.

**Sources:** Primary source for each commissioner is the USCC bio page at uscc.gov/commission-members/[name]; supplementary sources include AEI, Princeton, JHU SAIS, Hoover Institution, Wikipedia, and major news coverage. All commissioner roles and appointing authorities verified against uscc.gov as of April 2026.

**A note on the appointment structure:** The commission has 12 members appointed for 2-year terms, 3 each by the Senate Majority Leader, Senate Minority Leader, House Speaker, and House Minority Leader (per 22 U.S.C. § 7002 as amended). Members may be reappointed and frequently are. Chair and Vice Chair rotate; convention is that one is Republican-appointed and one Democratic-appointed.

## 2024 Report Cycle Commissioners

### Robin Cleveland — *Chair (2024 cycle); off commission*

Robin Cleveland served three decades in Republican national-security policy roles before joining USCC. Her career began on Senator Mitch McConnell's personal staff and continued through senior positions on the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, and as Clerk of the Foreign Operations Subcommittee of Senate Appropriations. In 2002, George W. Bush appointed her Associate Director for National Security and International Affairs at OMB, where she co-led the interagency efforts that established the Millennium Challenge Corporation and PEPFAR. In 2005 she moved to the World Bank as Counselor to President Paul Wolfowitz, where she handled debt-relief programs in Africa among a broader policy and budget portfolio. After leaving government she earned a PhD in Counseling and entered private practice as a psychotherapist; she remains adjunct faculty at George Washington University.

Cleveland was reappointed to USCC in 2022 by Senate Republican Leader McConnell for a term expiring December 31, 2024, and served as Chair throughout the 2024 report cycle. She had served on USCC since 2009 (per her opening statement: "I am deeply grateful that he appointed me to the Commission in 2009"), so the 2024 report was the capstone of nearly fifteen years of service. As of April 2026, she is no longer on the commission.

For advocacy purposes, Cleveland is the institutional voice behind the 2024 report's introduction and the unanimous-bipartisan transmittal letter. Her stated framing in the 2024 opening statement emphasized China's "aggressive export-led growth model" and the need for export controls, ICTS import controls, outbound investment review, and inter-agency coordination. She did not personally take an unusually strong position on AGI specifically — the Manhattan Project recommendation should be understood as something she signed onto unanimously rather than something she championed. She is the most "establishment-Republican" of the 2024 commissioners, with deep ties to the McConnell wing of the GOP rather than the MAGA wing.

### Reva Price — *Vice Chair (2024); Member (2026)*

Reva Price served seventeen-plus years as Director of Outreach and Senior Advisor to former Speaker Nancy Pelosi, handling key aspects of foreign-policy portfolios with particular emphasis on China and the Middle East. Her role spanned domestic and foreign policy issues and included building relationships with non-governmental organizations, coalitions, and advocacy groups. She is the Democratic House counterpart to Cleveland's Republican Senate institutionalist profile — a long-tenured staff principal with deep policy experience and bipartisan credibility, rather than a public-facing advocate for any particular ideological position.

Price was reappointed to USCC by House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries for a term expiring December 31, 2026, and continues to serve on the 2026 commission. She served as Vice Chair during the 2024 cycle and is now a regular member; the Vice Chair role rotated to Mike Kuiken (also Schumer-appointed) for 2026.

For advocacy purposes, Price is one of the more accessible 2024 commissioners — her career has been in coalition-building and outreach, which means she's likely to be more receptive than most to civil-society engagement on AI policy. Her China-policy positions are not extensively documented in public writing (unlike Friedberg, Brands, Helberg), so an advocate engaging her should expect to meet someone who has spent more time listening to subject-matter experts than developing personal positions in print. That makes her potentially persuadable in either direction, depending on which experts get to her.

### Hon. Randall Schriver — *Member (2024); Chair (2026)*

Randall Schriver chairs the Board of the Institute for Indo-Pacific Security and is a partner at Pacific Solutions LLC. He served in the first Trump administration as Assistant Secretary of Defense for Indo-Pacific Security Affairs (2018–2020), the principal advisor to the Secretary of Defense on Indo-Pacific matters, leading roughly 100 policy professionals. Earlier he served as Deputy Assistant Secretary of State for East Asian and Pacific Affairs under George W. Bush. He sits on the Board of Advisors of Sasakawa Peace Foundation USA, the Board of Directors of the US-Taiwan Business Council, and is a lecturer in Stanford's "Stanford-in-Washington" program. The "Hon." prefix reflects his Senate-confirmed Pentagon role.

Schriver was a regular commissioner during the 2024 cycle and was reappointed by Senate Republican Leader John Thune for a term expiring December 31, 2027 — and elevated to **Chair** for the 2026 cycle. This is institutionally significant: the 2026 report (due late 2026) will be issued under his name, not Cleveland's, and his Indo-Pacific defense background is a stronger steer toward Taiwan-defense and PLA-modernization framing than Cleveland's broader strategic-economic frame. He is widely respected on the Republican defense side and has been cited by Helberg and other tech-hawks as an authority on PRC military modernization.

For advocacy purposes, Schriver is the most defense-industrial-base-aligned of the senior commissioners. His 2024 hearing co-chair role for the China's Counter-Intervention Capabilities hearing (the basis of Chapter 8) and his US-Taiwan Business Council board seat give him strong views on PLA capabilities and Taiwan deterrence. He is unlikely to soften the 2024 report's hawkish framing in 2026; if anything, the Taiwan and counter-intervention chapters will get sharper. However, he has not personally been an outspoken AGI-acceleration advocate the way Helberg has, so the Manhattan Project recommendation is not necessarily safe in its 2024 form.

### Mike Kuiken — *Member (2024); Vice Chair (2026)*

Mike Kuiken is a Distinguished Visiting Fellow at Stanford's Hoover Institution and Managing Member of Silver Valley Strategies, a geopolitical advisory firm. He spent nearly 23 years on Capitol Hill, most recently as National Security Advisor to Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer — the Senate's most senior national-security staff role and a "Gang of Eight" position for over seven years. Earlier he was a senior staff member on the Senate Armed Services Committee for over twelve years. His most consequential legislative work was architecting the strategy that passed the CHIPS and Science Act, and helping establish the Senate's bipartisan AI Insight Forums under Schumer in 2023. He sits on the boards of the National Defense Industrial Association and the Planetary Science Institute.

Kuiken was reappointed by Senate Democratic Leader Schumer for a term expiring December 31, 2027, and was elevated to Vice Chair for the 2026 cycle. Educationally — Calvin University, per LinkedIn — and via his Schumer staff role, he comes from the technocratic-pragmatist Democratic foreign-policy tradition rather than the progressive wing.

**For your advocacy specifically, Kuiken is the most relevant commissioner.** He is a member of **Anthropic's National Security and Public Sector Advisory Council** and an advisor to the Special Competitive Studies Project (SCSP — the Eric Schmidt-funded successor to NSCAI, which has been broadly aligned with U.S. AI-leadership-with-safety framing). The combination of (a) Vice Chair status on the 2026 commission, (b) Anthropic NSPS Advisory Council membership, (c) CHIPS Act architect, and (d) AI Insight Forums architect makes him uniquely positioned to either advance or temper an AI-safety-aware version of USCC's tech-policy recommendations. A serious advocacy effort on the 2026 report should attempt to engage him directly through Anthropic's advisory-council channels if those are accessible, or through Hoover or SCSP otherwise. There may be a real conflict-of-interest discussion worth having, given that Anthropic is a frontier AI lab whose practices the commission is implicitly evaluating; that conflict has not been publicly aired and could become a story. Worth knowing about.

### Aaron Friedberg — *Member (2024); off commission*

Aaron Friedberg is Professor of Politics and International Affairs at Princeton (since 1987), co-director of Princeton's Center for International Security Studies, and a non-resident senior fellow at the American Enterprise Institute. From 2003 to 2005 he served as Deputy Assistant for National Security Affairs and Director of Policy Planning in the office of Vice President Cheney. He held the inaugural Henry A. Kissinger Chair at the Library of Congress (2001–02) and was subsequently appointed to the Defense Policy Board and the Secretary of State's Advisory Committee on Democracy Promotion. His 2011 book *A Contest for Supremacy: China, America and the Struggle for Mastery in Asia* (W.W. Norton) is one of the more influential academic statements of the China-as-strategic-rival thesis. He co-founded the Alexander Hamilton Society in 2010. AB and PhD from Harvard, in government.

Friedberg was reappointed by Senate Republican Leader McConnell for a term that expired December 31, 2025. He served on the commission from 2022 to 2024 (per AEI's bio), so the 2024 report was his second annual cycle; per earlier 2023-cycle hearing rosters he was active throughout. He is no longer on the commission as of 2026.

For advocacy purposes, Friedberg is the academic-realist anchor of the 2024 report. His scholarly position has migrated over decades from cautious engagement (his 1993 *International Security* article advocated continued U.S. engagement in East Asia until institutions and integration could mature) to more skeptical assessment of Chinese revisionism. He signed a 2020 letter of 130+ former Republican national-security officials declaring Trump unfit, which puts him in the "never-Trump but China-hawk" camp; that's a meaningful distinction from Helberg, Sims, and Hodges, who are MAGA-aligned. The Manhattan Project recommendation is more in tension with Friedberg's scholarly framing (engagement-failed-but-careful-analysis-required) than it is with Helberg's. His departure from the commission removes one of the more analytically careful voices.

### Kimberly T. Glas — *Member (2024); off commission*

Kim Glas is President and CEO of the National Council of Textile Organizations (NCTO) since April 2019, representing domestic textile and apparel manufacturers. Her career has been at the intersection of trade policy and labor/manufacturing advocacy: Deputy Assistant Secretary for Textiles, Consumer Goods, and Materials at Commerce under Obama (2009–2014), where she chaired the Committee for the Implementation of Textile Agreements; Executive Director of the BlueGreen Alliance (a labor/environmental coalition) before NCTO; and earlier Deputy Chief of Staff and Legislative Director for Rep. Mike Michaud (D-ME) plus senior staff for Rep. John LaFalce (D-NY). She helped found the bipartisan House Trade Working Group. BA in History (summa cum laude), SUNY Geneseo. She is a Schumer appointee and a labor-aligned trade Democrat.

Glas served as Vice Chair of the 2022 report cycle. She was reappointed by Schumer for a term ending December 31, 2024, which means the November 2024 report was her last vote — she is no longer on the commission.

For advocacy purposes, Glas is the strongest 2024 commissioner on supply-chain and de minimis loophole issues — she is openly celebratory about the de minimis closure that her industry lobbied for. Her perspective on AI is likely filtered through the labor/manufacturing lens (jobs and industrial policy) rather than the safety lens, which means Recommendation 1 (AGI Manhattan Project) probably appealed to her on industrial-policy grounds rather than capability-race grounds. Her departure removes a labor-coalition voice from the commission — none of the 2026 new appointees have a comparable union-aligned background.

### Hon. Carte P. Goodwin — *Member (2024); off commission*

Carte Goodwin is a partner-in-charge of the Charleston, West Virginia office of Frost Brown Todd, vice chair of the firm's national Appellate Practice Group, and serves as Chair of the West Virginia Bar Appellate Committee. He briefly served as a U.S. Senator from West Virginia in 2010 (July–November), appointed by then-Governor Joe Manchin to fill the seat after Senator Robert Byrd's death; he was the youngest Senator at the time. Earlier he was General Counsel to Governor Manchin (2005–2009), drafted West Virginia's mine-safety reforms following the Aracoma and Sago Mine accidents, and chaired the Independent Commission on Judicial Reform alongside former Justice Sandra Day O'Connor. JD from Emory Law (1999, Order of the Coif), BA from Marietta College. He clerked for Judge Robert B. King on the Fourth Circuit. The "Hon." prefix is for his Senate service.

Goodwin served on USCC from 2011 to 2021, then was reappointed by Senate Democratic Leader Schumer for a term ending December 31, 2025. He co-chaired the 2024 hearing on China's Stockpiling and Mobilization Measures (basis of Chapter 7). He is no longer on the commission as of 2026.

For advocacy purposes, Goodwin is the West Virginia center-left lawyer profile — an Appalachian Democrat with strong rule-of-law/judicial-reform orientation rather than a tech-policy background. His 2024 hearing co-chair role pulled him into the stockpiling/mobilization chapter, which is the most "preparing for war" framing in the report. His departure further reduces the legal-process voice on the commission.

### Jacob Helberg — *Member (2024); off commission, now Under Secretary of State*

Jacob Helberg, age 36, has had a remarkable trajectory from technology executive to Under Secretary of State. He was Google's global lead for Search policy (2016–2020), focused on combating foreign election interference, then Senior Advisor to the CEO of Palantir Technologies (Alex Karp) and Adjunct Senior Fellow at CNAS. He authored *The Wires of War: Technology and the Global Struggle for Power* (Simon & Schuster, October 2021). He founded the Hill & Valley Forum, a bipartisan coalition of Silicon Valley executives and U.S. lawmakers that has become an institutional locus for tech-China policy networking. Educationally, GWU undergrad and NYU Tandon MS in Cybersecurity Risk & Strategy (2020). He was a leading advocate for the 2024 TikTok ban legislation. **He is the spouse of investor Keith Rabois**, with the wedding officiated by Sam Altman in 2018 — an immediate-family connection to OpenAI's CEO that is worth knowing.

Helberg was appointed by Speaker Kevin McCarthy in 2022 for a term expiring December 31, 2024. He served on the 2024 report and is widely understood to be the principal author/champion of Recommendation 1 (the AGI Manhattan Project). On December 10, 2024, President-elect Trump nominated him as Under Secretary of State for Economic Growth, Energy, and the Environment; he was confirmed in October 2025 and is now serving as the 22nd Under Secretary in that role. In December 2025 he launched **Pax Silica**, a multilateral coalition described as the State Department's flagship effort on AI and supply chain security, with initial signatories Australia, Greece, Israel, Japan, Qatar, Republic of Korea, Singapore, UAE, UK, and Sweden.

For advocacy purposes, **Helberg is the single most consequential figure in U.S. AI–China policy and arguably in U.S. AI policy generally as of 2026.** He is no longer on USCC, but the AGI Manhattan Project recommendation he championed is now being operationalized in a different form through Pax Silica. The ideas overlap substantially: U.S. AI leadership, allied coalition, semiconductor/compute focus, supply-chain-security framing. The political coalition he built (Hill & Valley Forum, AFPI Center for American Security) is now at the center of Trump-administration AI policy. Any substantive AI-safety advocacy needs to engage with Helberg's frame — agreeing where possible (allied coalition, supply-chain security), pressing where necessary (the absence of safety evaluation in the AGI-acquisition framing). His personal connection to Sam Altman through the wedding officiant relationship is in the public record but does not appear to have been a published policy issue; treat it as background context, not as a charge.

### Leland R. Miller — *Member (2024 and 2026)*

Leland Miller is co-founder and CEO of China Beige Book, which since 2010 has run the world's largest private macroeconomic survey of China's economy — quarterly survey of thousands of Chinese firms across sectors, used by hedge funds and policymakers as an alternative to official Chinese economic statistics. He is a frequent commentator on CNBC, Bloomberg TV, CNN, and Fox Business, and his work is featured in the WSJ, NYT, FT, and Washington Post. Before founding China Beige Book he was a capital markets attorney based in New York and Hong Kong and worked on the deal team at a major investment bank. He's an elected life member of the Council on Foreign Relations and Executive-in-Residence at Washington & Lee's Williams School (2015–).

Miller was reappointed by Speaker Mike Johnson for a term expiring December 31, 2027 — making him one of the five carryovers from the 2024 cycle to the 2026 cycle. He co-chaired the 2024 hearing on Key Economic Strategies (basis of Chapter 6).

For advocacy purposes, Miller is the China-economy data anchor of the commission. He is very public-facing, with his Bloomberg TV / CNBC appearances making him the most-quoted commissioner in financial press. His expertise is *what is actually happening in China's economy* (debt, property crisis, manufacturing output, employment indicators) rather than U.S.-policy advocacy per se. Politically he is a McCarthy/Johnson appointee but his economic analysis has been used by both administrations. He is unlikely to push tech-policy recommendations himself but is heavily relied on by other commissioners for the macro framing that justifies their tech-policy recommendations. The "China's economic problems force pivot to high-tech industrial policy" framing in the 2024 report's introduction is largely his analytical territory.

### Cliff Sims — *Member (2024); off commission*

Cliff Sims is a Birmingham, Alabama-based Republican communications strategist and former senior White House and intelligence-community official. He served as Special Assistant to President Trump and Director of White House Message Strategy in the first administration (2017–18), then as Deputy Director of National Intelligence for Strategy and Communications under DNI John Ratcliffe (2020–21). He authored *Team of Vipers: My 500 Extraordinary Days in the Trump White House* (2019, NYT bestseller) and *The Darkness Has Not Overcome: Lessons on Faith and Politics from Inside the Halls of Power* (2024). After the 2024 election, he led the Trump Transition for the Central Intelligence Agency. He is a Senior Fellow at the America First Policy Institute's Center for American Security and chairman of Telegraph Creative (a Birmingham marketing firm). BA in Political Science (magna cum laude), University of Alabama; Executive Certificate in Public Leadership, Harvard Kennedy School.

Sims was named to USCC in February 2024 by Speaker Mike Johnson, alongside Leland Miller, for a term that has since ended. He co-chaired the 2024 hearing on China's Stockpiling and Mobilization Measures (basis of Chapter 7) alongside Goodwin.

For advocacy purposes, Sims is the MAGA-Trump-aligned commissioner on the 2024 report — his career and ideological positioning are continuous with Project 2025-aligned Republican messaging. Axios characterized him at his appointment as "one of Trumpworld's most vocal and aggressive China hawks." He worked closely with Ratcliffe on the controversial late-Trump claim that China sought to influence the 2020 election to defeat Trump — a claim that did not survive Biden-administration intelligence reviews and that you should expect to see as a credibility issue if his commission record gets examined. His recent CIA Transition leadership and AFPI Center for American Security role suggest he is now operating as a Trump-administration intelligence-policy player rather than as an independent analyst.

### Hon. Jonathan N. Stivers — *Member (2024 and 2026)*

Jon Stivers is the U.S. Director of the Committee for Freedom in Hong Kong (CFHK) Foundation. He recently served as the Democratic Staff Director on the House Select Committee on the Strategic Competition Between the U.S. and the Chinese Communist Party — the body that produced the December 2023 report on "Reset, Prevent, Build" U.S.-China policy. Earlier he was a Professional Staff Member on the House State-Foreign Operations Appropriations Subcommittee, and Staff Director of the Congressional-Executive Commission on China. In the Obama administration he was USAID Assistant Administrator for the Bureau for Asia, managing $1.2B in foreign assistance and 1,200 development professionals across 32 countries in East/South/Central Asia. Before USAID he was a Senior Advisor to Speaker Pelosi on foreign policy and trade. The "Hon." prefix is for his USAID Assistant Administrator role (Senate-confirmed). He previously served on USCC 2017–2018, then was reappointed to a new term.

Stivers was reappointed by House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries for a term expiring December 31, 2027 — one of the five carryovers to the 2026 cycle. He co-chaired the 2024 hearing on China and the Middle East (basis of Chapter 5).

For advocacy purposes, Stivers is one of the most operationally experienced commissioners on actually-implementing-policy-affecting-China — his CECC and Select Committee staff roles have given him close visibility into legislative drafting and his USAID role gave him implementation experience. He is Hong Kong-focused via the CFHK Foundation, which means the Hong Kong chapter (Ch 10) was likely his domain in the 2024 report. He is generally aligned with the human-rights-and-democracy framing of China policy more than the techno-industrial framing, which makes him a potential ally for AI-safety arguments grounded in democracy/civil-liberties concerns rather than competitive-economic concerns. His continuation onto the 2026 commission preserves one of the more rule-of-law-oriented voices.

### Michael R. Wessel — *Member (2024); off commission*

Mike Wessel is President of The Wessel Group Incorporated (a public-affairs consulting firm) and an original member of USCC since its 2000 founding. He served on the staff of former House Democratic Leader Richard Gephardt for over two decades — General Counsel, chief policy advisor, principal Ways and Means aide, tax policy coordinator to the 1990 budget summit, and senior strategist on every major tax bill of the period. He served as national issues director for Gephardt's 1988 presidential campaign and senior policy advisor to Clinton's 1992 transition; trade/economic policy advisor to Kerry 2004, Obama 2008, Clinton 2016, and Biden 2020 campaigns. He also served on the U.S. Trade Deficit Review Commission (1999–2000), the predecessor body whose staff and facilities were absorbed into USCC. JD and BA from George Washington University; member of the DC and Pennsylvania bars; member of the Council on Foreign Relations.

Wessel was reappointed by then-Speaker Pelosi for a term that expired December 31, 2024 — meaning the November 2024 report was his last cycle after 24 years on USCC. He is the longest-tenured commissioner in USCC history. He co-chaired the 2024 hearing on Key Economic Strategies (basis of Chapter 6) alongside Miller.

For advocacy purposes, Wessel is the institutional memory of USCC and the labor-Democrat trade-policy anchor on the 2024 commission. His departure is the single largest loss of institutional continuity in the commission's history. Politically he is to the left of Glas on labor/trade issues and considerably more skeptical of free-trade orthodoxy than Cleveland or Friedberg. He is unlikely to have personally championed the AGI Manhattan Project recommendation; his sign-on probably reflects general agreement with the U.S.-leadership frame rather than enthusiasm for AGI specifically. His departure removes the last commissioner with deep direct ties to Democratic congressional trade-policy networks; replacement Chris Slevin is a partial substitute (Brown/Booker staffer, Commerce/CHIPS Act experience) but doesn't carry the same multi-decade political weight.

## 2026 Report Cycle — New Members

### Dr. Hal Brands

Hal Brands is the Henry A. Kissinger Distinguished Professor of Global Affairs at Johns Hopkins SAIS (since 2016), a senior fellow at AEI, and a Bloomberg Opinion columnist. He served as Special Assistant to the Secretary of Defense for Strategic Planning (2015–16) and was lead writer for the Commission on the National Defense Strategy (2017–18 and 2024). He's a member of the Secretary of State's Foreign Affairs Policy Board and a senior advisor at Macro Advisory Partners. His books include *Danger Zone: The Coming Conflict with China* (with Michael Beckley, 2022), *The Twilight Struggle: What the Cold War Teaches Us about Great-Power Rivalry Today* (2022), *American Grand Strategy in the Age of Trump* (2018), and the recent *The Eurasian Century* (2025). PhD, MA, MPhil from Yale (history); BA Stanford. His father is the historian H. W. Brands. He's roughly 42 years old.

Brands was appointed by Senate Republican Leader Mitch McConnell for a term beginning January 2025, expiring December 31, 2026 — joining the commission for the 2026 cycle.

For advocacy purposes, Brands is the academic-strategic-studies anchor of the 2026 commission, in the role Friedberg occupied for 2024. Brands is somewhat more publicly aggressive than Friedberg on China — *Danger Zone* argues that the 2020s are a particularly dangerous "window" for U.S.-China conflict because Chinese power may peak before the U.S. responds, and policy should be calibrated to that window. The "Danger Zone" thesis has been influential in defense circles and is largely compatible with the 2024 commission's hawkish framing. Brands is not a tech-specific commentator, so AI policy is not his lane — expect him to defer to other commissioners on AI and lead on grand-strategy framing. His McConnell rather than Trump appointment suggests he is positioned as Senate-Republican-establishment rather than MAGA-aligned, similar to Friedberg's positioning.

### Taylor Budowich

Taylor Budowich is the president of The Sovereign Advisors, a DC-based public-affairs and crisis-communications firm. Most recently he served as Deputy White House Chief of Staff and Cabinet Secretary in the second Trump administration, overseeing strategic messaging, media operations, and the President's Cabinet. He founded and led MAGA Inc. (2023–24), the principal Super PAC supporting Trump's 2024 campaign, and was Director of Communications for President Trump in 2021–22. In 2018 he was a senior advisor for Ron DeSantis's first gubernatorial campaign; before that he led Tea Party Express as Executive Director.

Budowich was appointed by Speaker Mike Johnson for a term expiring December 31, 2027 — a new commissioner for the 2026 cycle.

For advocacy purposes, Budowich is the most narrowly Trump-political of the 2026 commissioners. His career has been as a Republican campaign operative and message strategist; he has no documented expertise in China policy, U.S.-China economic relations, technology, or national security. His appointment is an unusually political pick relative to the commission's traditional staffing pattern — historically, even partisan appointees have come with subject-matter credentials (Helberg's Palantir/CNAS background, Sims's intelligence-community role, Hodges's NSC role). The signal here is that Speaker Johnson is treating one of his three USCC seats as a political-influence seat rather than a subject-matter seat, similar to how some administrations have used certain commission appointments. Engaging Budowich on substantive AI policy will likely require working through his political-strategy lens rather than expecting a substantive-policy lens.

### Joshua Hodges

Josh Hodges currently serves as Senior Director of Corporate Affairs and Senior Adviser to the Chair at VEON Ltd., a multinational telecommunications and digital services company. Previously he was National Security Advisor to Speaker Mike Johnson (R-LA). He served as Special Assistant to President Trump and Senior Director at the National Security Council in the first administration. He has held senior roles at the Inter-American Development Bank, the National Nuclear Security Administration, both the House and Senate Judiciary Committees, and was Military Legislative Assistant to Sen. David Vitter (R-LA).

Hodges was appointed by Speaker Johnson for a term expiring December 31, 2026 — a new commissioner for the 2026 cycle.

For advocacy purposes, Hodges has the deepest national-security-staffer profile of the 2026 newcomers — NSC, NNSA, IDB, Senate Judiciary, congressional-defense roles. He is positioned to be Speaker Johnson's substantive-policy voice on the commission, parallel to how Helberg (Pelosi-then-McCarthy era) and Sims (Johnson era) operated. His VEON role is worth noting: VEON operates telecommunications networks in Russia, Ukraine, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and several other emerging markets where Chinese telecom infrastructure (Huawei, ZTE) competes — so his commercial-sector vantage on Chinese telecom expansion in the global south is a relevant qualification. Less is known about his views on AI specifically; treat him as a likely defense-and-security-framing commissioner rather than a tech-policy specialist.

### Livia Shmavonian

Livia Shmavonian most recently was Made in America Director at OMB under President Biden, leading the executive branch's Buy American/Made in America procurement push. Earlier she was Director of Legislative and Intergovernmental Affairs and Senior Advisor to the Under Secretary for International Trade at the Commerce Department. On Capitol Hill she was Staff Director for the Senate Finance Subcommittee on International Trade, Customs, and Global Competitiveness, and Senior Advisor to former Sen. Bob Casey (D-PA).

Shmavonian was appointed by Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer for a term expiring December 31, 2026 — a new commissioner for the 2026 cycle.

For advocacy purposes, Shmavonian is the labor-and-industrial-policy-focused Democrat on the 2026 commission, in roughly the role that Glas and Wessel occupied for 2024. Her Made in America Director role at OMB and her Senate Finance Trade Subcommittee staff role gave her unusual depth on procurement, trade, and U.S. industrial-policy mechanics. She is unlikely to be the AI-policy lead but is well-positioned to handle the supply-chain/critical-minerals/industrial-policy framing that is increasingly converging with AI policy (rare-earth minerals for chips, fab siting, etc.). Her Schumer appointment alongside Kuiken means Schumer's two seats are now substantive economic-policy professionals — a change from the Schumer-appointed seats during the 2024 cycle (Glas, Kuiken, Goodwin) which were a more diverse set.

### Chris Slevin

Chris Slevin most recently was Chief of Staff at the U.S. Department of Commerce under Secretary Gina Raimondo, where he led the agency through the CHIPS and Science Act implementation and the agency's expanded technology/national-security role (described as "a nearly ten-fold budget increase" in his uscc.gov bio — a measure of how much CHIPS Act and tech-policy work expanded Commerce's footprint). Earlier President Biden appointed him Deputy Assistant to the President for Legislative Affairs, where his portfolio included the bipartisan infrastructure legislation, veterans-health legislation, and the CHIPS Act. He served on the Hill as senior policy advisor to Senators Sherrod Brown (D-OH) and Cory Booker (D-NJ). Earlier he was Vice President at the Economic Innovation Group and started in policy work at Public Citizen as communications and deputy director of its global trade division.

Slevin was appointed by House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries for a term expiring December 31, 2026 — a new commissioner for the 2026 cycle.

For advocacy purposes, **Slevin is the second-most-relevant commissioner for AI-safety advocacy after Kuiken**. As Raimondo's Chief of Staff he had visibility into NIST AI Safety Institute setup, BIS export-control mechanics, and the CHIPS Act implementation — the entire executive-branch AI/tech-policy infrastructure. His career trajectory (Public Citizen → progressive Senate Democrats → EIG → Biden administration → USCC) suggests he is more progressive than Kuiken on regulatory questions and more skeptical of pure techno-competitive framings. The combination of Slevin and Kuiken on the Democratic side of the 2026 commission means there are two commissioners with deep CHIPS Act/AI-policy implementation experience, which is a meaningful upgrade in technical depth on AI-related questions. The specific question worth investigating: did Slevin work on the NIST AI Safety Institute policy framing, and what does he think of its work? That answer would tell you a lot about how the 2026 report will treat domestic AI safety governance.

## Cross-cutting observations

**The 2026 commission is more technically credentialed on AI/tech policy than the 2024 commission.** Adding Brands (Defense Strategy Commission writer, geopolitics scholar), Hodges (NSC and telecom-industry experience), Shmavonian (Made in America, Commerce trade), and especially Slevin (Raimondo Commerce CoS, CHIPS Act implementation) — and elevating Kuiken (CHIPS Act architect, AI Insight Forums architect, Anthropic NSPS) to Vice Chair — gives the 2026 commission substantially more direct technology-policy implementation experience than the 2024 commission had. This is not the same as being safety-aligned, but it does mean the 2026 commission is better-positioned to write recommendations that interact intelligently with existing executive-branch tech-policy machinery.

**Helberg's departure to State and his Pax Silica initiative effectively continues the 2024 commission's tech-policy vision through different institutional channels.** AGI Manhattan Project as a USCC recommendation is now somewhat orphaned — its primary champion is no longer on the commission — but the underlying agenda (allied AI/compute coalition, supply-chain security, accelerated U.S. AI leadership) is now executive-branch policy via Pax Silica. Treat the 2024 USCC recommendation and the 2025–26 Pax Silica activities as one coherent policy push that has shifted institutional venues, not as separate things.

**The Anthropic-Kuiken connection is the single most important institutional fact for your specific advocacy.** It is also a fact that has not been publicly examined as a conflict-of-interest question. Kuiken is the Vice Chair of the body that recommends to Congress how to regulate, fund, or restrict frontier AI development; he is also on the advisory council of one of the frontier AI labs whose practices the commission is implicitly evaluating. This is worth knowing about, worth thinking carefully about how to engage, and worth being aware that someone else (a journalist, a hostile commissioner, etc.) may eventually surface it as a story.

**The Republican side of the 2026 commission is more establishment-leaning than the 2024 side.** Brands and Schriver are McConnell/Thune-tradition Senate Republicans; Hodges is a Speaker Johnson NSC-experienced staffer rather than a MAGA messaging operative; Budowich is an exception. Sims and Helberg, the two most aggressive 2024 commissioners on China rhetoric, have both rotated off. Whether this means a softer 2026 report or just a less rhetorically aggressive one is yet to be determined, but the personnel shift is real.

**The Democratic side of the 2026 commission is more economic-policy-credentialed than the 2024 side.** Adding Slevin (Raimondo CoS) and Shmavonian (OMB Made in America) and elevating Kuiken (Schumer NSA) shifts the Democratic side from political-coalition-and-labor (Wessel, Price, Glas, Goodwin) toward executive-branch-and-trade-policy implementation. This is a meaningful shift in the kind of expertise the Democratic appointees bring.

**Five carryovers preserve continuity.** Schriver, Kuiken, Miller, Price, Stivers all serve on both reports. Their views and frame will be the strongest single influence on how 2024-report recommendations carry forward into the 2026 cycle. If you want to predict what the 2026 commission will say about AGI/AI, watch what these five say about the 2024 recommendations between now and December 2026.
