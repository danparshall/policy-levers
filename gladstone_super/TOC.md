## 1. Executive Summary — *p. 3*

Five challenge categories: supply chain, model developer, loss of control, oversight, plus a closing argument that security can't be bolted on later.

## 2. Introduction — *p. 6*

The framing chapter. Asserts the load-bearing claim: U.S. frontier labs are almost certainly CCP-penetrated, therefore the U.S. has no real lead in the AI race, therefore the question is not whether to launch a national project but how.

- **Scope of the investigation** — *p. 10* — explicitly disclaims debating *whether* to nationalize; focuses on the implementation-level problems any USG-backed project would have to solve.

## 3. Data center security — *p. 12*

The longest chapter (18 pp). The operational core of the document. Each subsection closes with a recommendation block.

- **Physical security** — *p. 13* — special-forces red-teaming finds $20–30K attacks that take down $2B facilities for 6+ months.
- **Hardware supply chain security** — *p. 15* — transformers, BMCs, PLCs; ASPEED Taiwan makes 70% of the world's BMC chips.
- **Side-channel security** — *p. 19* — TEMPEST and Security Level 4/5 thresholds; new builds required (no retrofits possible).
- **Software supply chain security** — *p. 22* — software libraries used by frontier labs have been compromised; need DOD-style approved-libraries process.
- **Energy security** — *p. 23* — gas turbines as the near-term answer; environmental litigation framed as covert CCP attack vector.
- **Export controls** — *p. 25* — move from entity blacklists to selective whitelists; close the 1,700-GPU "AI diffusion regulation" loophole.
- **AI hardware security** — *p. 26* — TSMC fabrication and CoWoS packaging concentration in Taiwan; Intel/Amkor as onshore alternatives.

## 4. AI model developer security — *p. 30*

Personnel, cyber, and emissions security at the frontier labs themselves — the source of the algorithmic insights, not the data centers.

- **Personnel security** — *p. 31* — MSS targets ethnic Chinese as policy; CCP escalation playbook; security clearance process inadequate.
- **Cybersecurity** — *p. 34* — anonymous OpenAI-researcher whistleblower account about an unaddressed weights-exfiltration vulnerability.
- **Emissions security** — *p. 36* — TEMPEST in dense urban areas; greenfield siting required.

## 5. AI control — *p. 38*

Loss-of-control as a near-term operational problem, not a long-term theoretical one. Key argument: control problems and security problems are inseparable (an adversary-trojaned model is functionally an uncontrolled model).

- **The current game plan** — *p. 41* — what the labs actually believe their alignment plan is, and why Gladstone judges it inadequate. Recommends an AI-control ombudsman with whole-project authority.

## 6. The chain of command — *p. 45*

The concentration-of-power chapter. Anchored on the nuclear-chain-of-command analogy, with the recognition that nuclear authority is also delegated downward (not always presidential). The unnamed-but-identifiable "pathological liar … sociopath" passage about a frontier-lab CEO is here.

## 7. Conclusion — *p. 50*

Five subsections that pull the document's strategic logic together.

- **Objective of the project** — *p. 51* — three concerns (weaponization / loss of control / power concentration) all push toward maximizing U.S. lead time; cooperation with China currently impossible due to trust gap.
- **Strategies** — *p. 53* — three pillars: (1) actively delay CCP, (2) secure existing labs, (3) advance controlled-superintelligence R&D — strictly in that order.
- **Project ownership** — *p. 56* — DOE most plausible, with DARPA / CYBERCOM / NSA / CIA support. Government should not run day-to-day.
- **Project funding** — *p. 57* — investor-return model; DOE direct funding for security/control/oversight; "no-year" funding to avoid CR exposure.
- **Security, espionage, and counterintelligence** — *p. 58* — the document's most operationally aggressive section: no version of a national project exists that isn't integrated with active offensive operations against adversaries from day one.
