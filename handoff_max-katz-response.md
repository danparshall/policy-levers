## Where to take the revised draft

The nuclear / abundance-economy angle is the right push. Max is ENR.
"Too cheap to meter" is a live policy conversation in 2026 as SMRs and
next-gen reactors are actually being sited, with AI compute demand as
one of the primary drivers. Heinrich himself is on record supporting
nuclear expansion.

Candidate revised structure:

1. **Open short.** Acknowledge the reason-backward challenge, name that
   the honest ENR-side ceiling is bigger than the DOE-compute-plus-
   retraining framing captured.
2. **Lead with abundance-economy reframe.** If we take the AI trajectory
   seriously, either (a) we get catastrophic outcomes where nothing else
   matters, or (b) we get an abundance economy where energy consumption
   is nearly synonymous with wealth. Under (b), federal energy policy
   today is setting the ceiling for human material flourishing in the
   2030s. That's the ENR ceiling-level frame.
3. **Operationalize via nuclear buildout at civilizational scale.** Not
   "some SMRs near data centers" — a deliberate federal strategy to
   return to "too cheap to meter" as the design target for a
   post-AI-productivity energy economy. DOE loan guarantees, federal
   off-take agreements, NRC regulatory reform, advanced reactor
   demonstration programs, fuel cycle infrastructure.
4. **Connect back to Max's framing.** This is what reasoning backward
   from "AI trajectory is serious" produces on the ENR side. The ceiling
   is not modest infrastructure investment; it's treating the next
   decade of energy policy as the foundation of whatever comes after
   mass productivity gains.
5. **Keep a shorter pin on the labor ceiling.** Brief mention that on
   Finance / HELP jurisdiction, the parallel ceiling is a modernized GI
   Bill funded by compute excise tax → sovereign wealth fund. Name it,
   don't elaborate.
6. **Close with offer.** Happy to develop either ceiling further if it
   would be useful — or to try a third framing if both are still too
   small.

## Important caveats on the nuclear framing

Don't let the abundance-economy lead accidentally commit Dan to a
techno-optimist identity that undermines his broader AI-risk work. The
framing should be: "IF the AI trajectory doesn't produce catastrophe,
energy is the binding constraint on the upside scenario. THEREFORE
federal energy policy sets the ceiling on the upside. THEREFORE we
should actually size it to that ceiling." This acknowledges
catastrophic risk while doing useful work in the non-catastrophic branch.

Don't:
- Promise "too cheap to meter" as achievable without qualification.
  Historically this phrase is cursed; using it unhedged reads as naive.
  Use it as an evocative design-target framing with appropriate hedges.
- Overclaim Dan's expertise on energy policy specifically. He's a
  physicist with general energy literacy, not a nuclear policy
  specialist. The response should pose the frame, not pretend to have
  all the technical details on NRC reform or SMR deployment timelines.
- Push a specific partisan policy package. The ENR committee is
  bipartisan-functional on energy infrastructure; the framing should
  work for both Heinrich and Republican ENR members.

## Tone notes

Max is a peer, not a gatekeeper. The prior draft opened "Fair
challenge" — that's approximately right. Avoid excessive gratitude ("I
really appreciate your pushback" is too much). Avoid hedging ("this is
probably not the right frame but..."). Max is a physics PhD who asked a

specific answer to engage with.

Length target: 4-6 paragraphs. Senior Hill people reward brevity. No
bullet lists. Active voice. One crisp citation if it helps — e.g., the
Alaska Permanent Fund precedent — but don't pile up references.
