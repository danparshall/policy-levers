## Front page — "The window for AI policy is measured in months"

**Header brand:** Canary Institute For AI Policy. Briefing attribution: "Daniel Parshall, Ph.D. · dan@canaryinstitute.ai"

### Public opinion (lead block)
- 73% of US adults support robust AI regulation; 12% oppose
- 64% believe superhuman AI should not be developed until proven safe and controllable, or should never be developed
- 69% say government is not doing enough to regulate AI
- **Sources:** FLI / YouGov October 2025 (n=2,000); Quinnipiac April 2025

### Capability trajectory
- Length of software tasks AI can complete with 50% reliability is **doubling every ~7 months** (METR, 2025)
- Across 3,000+ general employment tasks evaluated by domain experts, doubling time is **3.8 months**; frontier models in late 2024 already reached 50% success rate on tasks taking humans a full workday (MIT FutureTech; Mertens et al., 2026)
- Framing: "This is not a forecast; it's a measurement"

### Economic reach
- Canary Institute analysis of all 923 occupations in the federal labor database (Parshall & Lopez-Luzuriaga, 2026):
  - **40%** of US labor time is in tasks within current AI cognitive capability, executable on standard digital infrastructure, not subject to meaningful regulatory protection
  - Additional **20%** is technically within AI's reach but currently protected by professional licensure, federal statute, or human-accountability requirements

### Brookings (wage trajectory)
- Kording & Marinescu, November 2025 working paper, Brookings Center on Regulation and Markets
- Models wage trajectory under AI automation as **hump-shaped**: rising initially as productivity gains dominate, then falling as workers crowd into physical sector
- Policy recommendation: pace AI deployment to allow capital investment to catch up

### "What Congress can do now" — three explicit asks

1. **PREFERRED:** Oppose federal preemption of state AI safety laws. Cites the December 11, 2025 Executive Order directing federal agencies to challenge California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act and New York's RAISE Act. Notes Anthropic's CEO has called the proposed 10-year preemption moratorium "too blunt."

2. **MINIMUM:** Support binding pre-deployment safety evaluation of frontier AI models, as proposed in the **Hawley-Blumenthal Artificial Intelligence Risk Evaluation Act (S. 2938, introduced September 29, 2025)**. Frames as: first federal bill with explicit statutory language on AI loss-of-control scenarios and "scheming behavior"; currently stalled in Senate Commerce Committee.

3. **FALLBACK:** Sign a Dear Colleague letter to Chairman Cruz requesting a Commerce Committee hearing on S. 2938. Notes the bill has had no markup, no hearing, and no additional cosponsors in the seven months since introduction.

## Back page — "AI's reach into the U.S. economy"

### The chart (top half)
- Capability × Deployment-difficulty matrix
- Capability axis: C0 (lookup) → C4 (discovery)
- Deployment-difficulty axis: D0 (fully digital) → D4 (dynamic physical environment)
- Three nested envelopes showing expansion of AI's economic reach:
  - 2023 baseline (Eloundou et al.): **16.2%**
  - 2026 CDR Framework: **50.5%**
  - ~2028 projection (extrapolating METR + Epoch trends): **67.6%**
- CEO expectations: ~**2M jobs** to be cut (Yotzov et al., 2026, NBER WP 34836)
- **Source:** Parshall & Lopez-Luzuriaga (2026); METR (2025); Epoch AI (2026)

### "Leaders of every major AI company say their products could cause human extinction"
- May 2023 CAIS one-sentence statement signed by Amodei (Anthropic), Altman (OpenAI), Hassabis (DeepMind), Hinton, Bengio
- Amodei estimate: 10–25% catastrophic outcomes
- Hinton estimate: 10–20%
- Jones 2024 (American Economic Review: Insights), "The AI Dilemma": under standard risk-aversion assumptions, society should accept **no more than 4% extinction probability** in exchange for AI-driven growth
- Punchline: industry estimates exceed economically defensible threshold by 3-6×
- Jones 2026 (Journal of Economic Perspectives): AI safety spending underfunded by **30× or more**

### "These systems are grown, not programmed"
- Frontier models are not written line-by-line; behaviors emerge from gradient descent over trillions of parameters trained on internet-scale data
- Lack basic control mechanisms (predictable execution, traceable logic, debuggable code, formal specifications) of other critical-infrastructure software
- Even the developers cannot reliably specify, predict, or constrain what their products will do
- Boeing 737 MAX analogy: software guardrails added to compensate for engine-placement problem; two crashes, 346 deaths
- Direct claim: industry calls retrain-until-test-passes "alignment"
